You capture and link. Then what?

Most apps optimize for getting thoughts out of your head. That's the easy part.

Your notes pile up

You never look at them again

They don't change how you think

Some tools add linking—but connection-making alone isn't learning. You can have a beautiful knowledge graph and still never know anything.

The hard part is making ideas actually yours—integrating them into your mental models, retrieving them at the right moment, applying them without thinking.

Reading something doesn't mean understanding it

Writing something down doesn't mean remembering it

Having it doesn't mean using it

Real learning requires reflection, connection-making, spaced repetition, active recall—the hard, unglamorous work that turns information into internalized knowledge. If you're going to take notes at all, they should make you smarter over time. Flint is designed so they do.

The complete learning cycle

1

Externalize

Structured capture through note types and agent guidance

  • Note types encode different thinking patterns (daily, evergreen, predictions)
  • Agent suggests inline as you write, deepening thinking through semantic understanding
  • Metadata schemas per type guide what questions you ask as you capture

Outcome: You externalize not just what you're thinking, but you're thinking deeper in the moment of capture

2

Internalize

Routines, agent partnership, connections made visible

  • Routines systematize recurring reflection (daily reviews, weekly syntheses)
  • Agent understands note types and relationships, providing type-aware assistance
  • Wikilinks and backlinks make connections explicit and discoverable
  • Reference Shelf keeps related notes visible while you work, creating passive association

Outcome: You're not just storing knowledge—you're continuously synthesizing and connecting it

3

Resurface

Spaced repetition scheduling, forced recall, agent-generated prompts

  • Review system brings notes back at calculated intervals based on performance
  • Backlinks surface where ideas recur, forcing pattern recognition
  • Agent generates contextual prompts to test active recall (not passive rereading)
  • Routines surface your best ideas at intervals optimized for long-term retention

Outcome: Ideas become part of how you think, not just what you know

Deep thinking in action

Here's what the complete learning cycle looks like:

1

Capture

You read an article on a topic. You highlight a key passage in your Reading note, capture it with context.

2

Connect

Agent analyzes the note, suggests three related concepts from your existing evergreen notes.

3

Reflect

You create wikilinks to those concepts. A connection emerges you hadn't seen before—you capture a new insight.

4

Synthesize

Your weekly synthesis routine runs. It surfaces this reading and the connections you made, prompts you to synthesize into a bigger principle.

5

Resurface

Two weeks later, spaced repetition brings back that synthesized principle. Your brain must retrieve it from memory first, reinforcing the learning.

6

Realize

Days or weeks later, in conversation or on a new project, you realize you're applying this principle instinctively. It's become part of how you think.

## Opening (v3)

_[Note: Previous attempts felt too academic. Trying a more personal entry point.]_

I used to think creativity was something that happened to you. Like catching a cold, or falling in love - you couldn't force it, couldn't predict it, could only wait and hope it would arrive.

Then I spent a decade trying to write, and waiting stopped feeling like patience. It started feeling like avoidance.

The breakthrough came not from a burst of inspiration but from reading a book about thinking. Daniel Kahneman's _Thinking, Fast and Slow_ wasn't about creativity - it was about cognitive biases and decision-making. But buried in its pages was a key that unlocked something I'd been struggling to understand: we don't have one mind. We have two.

## The Two Systems

_[Note: This is where the research from [[Thinking, Fast and Slow]] comes in - need to translate academic concepts into narrative.]_

Kahneman calls them System 1 and System 2, names deliberately bland to avoid the baggage of older terms like "conscious" and "unconscious." But the distinction they capture is anything but bland.

**System 1** is fast, automatic, and effortless. It's the part of you that recognizes faces, catches a ball, reads this sentence without sounding out each word. It runs constantly, processing the world and presenting you with interpretations you never asked for.

**System 2** is slow, deliberate, and effortful. It's the part that does long division, compares insurance policies, writes a careful email to someone who matters. It feels like "you" in a way System 1 doesn't.

## What This Means for Making Things

_[This section is the crux - connecting Kahneman to creativity. Still rough.]_

Here's what I eventually realized: creative work requires both systems, but not in the way I expected.

System 2 can't generate creative ideas. Try it - sit down and _decide_ to have a brilliant insight. Force yourself to produce something original through pure effort. It doesn't work. System 2 is great at evaluating, comparing, refining. But it can't create from nothing.

System 1, on the other hand, creates constantly. It's always making connections, surfacing associations, generating possibilities. The problem is that most of what it produces is junk - clichés, random noise, the same tired patterns.

The art of creative work, I came to believe, is learning to **prepare System 1 well** (through reading, practice, and immersion) and then **getting System 2 out of the way** (through the strange discipline of not-forcing).

_[Note: This is where [[Bird by Bird]]'s "shitty first drafts" connects - it's about letting System 1 run.]_

## The Perfectionism Problem

_[Transition to why this is hard.]_

System 2 doesn't like stepping back. It wants to control, to judge, to fix problems before they fully emerge. This is useful in many areas of life - you don't want to send the first email that comes to mind, or blurt out whatever System 1 generates in conversation.

But in creative work, this caution becomes a trap. Anne Lamott calls it the voice of the oppressor - that internal critic that evaluates your words before they reach the page.
